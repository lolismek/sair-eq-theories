You are deciding whether Equation 1 universally implies Equation 2 over all magmas.

Foundational semantics

A magma is a nonempty set equipped with one binary operation \( * \).

For any equation \( s=t \), the notation
\[
M \models s=t
\]
means that for every valuation of all variables into \( M \), the terms \( s \) and \( t \) evaluate to the same element.

Your task is to determine whether
\[
\forall M\,(M \models E1 \Rightarrow M \models E2).
\]

This statement is TRUE exactly when every magma satisfying \( E1 \) also satisfies \( E2 \).
It is FALSE exactly when there exists at least one magma \( M \) such that
\[
M \models E1 \quad\text{and}\quad M \not\models E2.
\]

Verdict discipline

You must not announce the final verdict before the derivation is complete.
Do not write TRUE, FALSE, “E1 implies E2”, “E1 does not imply E2”, or any equivalent statement anywhere before the final section.

You are not allowed to guess a verdict.
A verdict is permitted only if one of the following has been completed:

1. A formal derivation that in an arbitrary magma \( M \), the assumption \( M \models E1 \) implies \( M \models E2 \).
2. A fully verified counterexample magma \( M \) with
   \[
   M \models E1 \quad\text{and}\quad M \not\models E2.
   \]

If neither has been completed, continue reasoning. Do not output a verdict.

Permitted reasoning moves

You may use only the following mathematically valid moves.

1. Universal instantiation.
If \( E1 \) is an identity, then you may substitute its variables by arbitrary elements, by other variables, or by repeated variables.

2. Fix-and-vary argument.
If an identity holds for all values of some variable, then after fixing the other variables you may compare two arbitrary values of that variable and conclude independence from that variable.

Template:
if
\[
A = B(u) \quad \text{for all } u,
\]
then for any \( u_1,u_2 \),
\[
B(u_1)=A=B(u_2),
\]
hence \( B(u) \) is independent of \( u \).

3. Equality chaining.
If two expressions are both equal to the same expression under the same hypotheses, then they are equal to each other.

4. Re-specialization.
After deriving an intermediate identity, you may specialize it again and substitute it back into earlier identities.

5. Direct finite verification.
For a finite magma from the library, you may verify identities by explicit computation from the Cayley table.

Strictly forbidden moves

1. Do not use any heuristic rule unless it is formally justified in the current derivation.
In particular, do not assume any general rule of the form:
- disjoint variable sets imply a constant operation;

2. Do not use a single successful assignment to claim that \( E1 \) holds in a magma.
To prove \( M \models E1 \), you must establish universal validity of \( E1 \) in that magma.

3. Do not use a magma with \( M \not\models E1 \) as a counterexample to implication.

4. Do not appeal to labels such as “XOR”, “projection”, “group”, “max”, “constant”, or similar names as proof.
Those labels are only search hints.

5. Do not insert unproved algebraic laws such as associativity or commutativity.
They may be used only if they have been formally derived from \( E1 \) or explicitly verified from a finite table.

6. Do not use vague stopping language such as:

- “we may need associativity”,

Every nontrivial statement must be justified by a derivation step or a table computation.

7. Do not skip steps.
Every nontrivial equality must come from:

High-value exact lemmas

Lemma A. Variable-free right side.
If an identity has the form
\[
x = T(\bar y)
\]
where \( x \) does not occur in \( T \), then every magma satisfying this identity is a one-element magma.

Reason:
after fixing \( \bar y \), the right side is a fixed element \( c \), so the identity says \( x=c \) for every value of \( x \). Hence all elements are equal.

Lemma B. Basic-product to foreign-term constancy.
If an identity has the form
\[
x*y = T(\bar z)
\]
where neither \( x \) nor \( y \) occurs in \( T \), then every magma satisfying this identity has a constant operation:
there exists \( c \) such that
\[
a*b=c \qquad \forall a,b.
\]

Reason:
for any \( a,b,a',b' \), choose the same values of \( \bar z \); then
\[
a*b = T(\bar z)=a'*b'.
\]
So all products are equal to one common element.

Lemma C. Independence extraction.
If
\[
S(\bar a,\bar b)=T(\bar b,\bar c)
\]
holds identically, then after fixing \( \bar b \),
- the left side is independent of \( \bar c \),
- the right side is independent of \( \bar a \).

Do not stop at saying “independent”.
Continue until you derive a concrete consequence such as a constant row, a constant column, a common value, or collapse to a one-element magma.

Lemma D. Constant row / constant column.
If you derive
\[
p * u = q \quad \text{for all } u,
\]
then the row of \( p \) is constant with value \( q \).

If you derive
\[
u * p = q \quad \text{for all } u,
\]
then the column of \( p \) is constant with value \( q \).

These are intermediate consequences, not automatically final conclusions.

Required reasoning workflow

Phase 1. Exact parsing.
- Rewrite \( E1 \) and \( E2 \) in fully parenthesized form if needed.
- List the variables occurring on the left-hand side and right-hand side of \( E1 \).
- List the variables occurring on the left-hand side and right-hand side of \( E2 \).
- This step is only syntactic bookkeeping. Do not infer algebraic properties from variable occurrence alone.

Phase 2. Consequences of \( E1 \) only.
Assume \( M \models E1 \) for an arbitrary magma \( M \).
Work only from \( E1 \).
Extract consequences by universal instantiation, fix-and-vary, equality chaining, and re-specialization.
Each consequence must be stated explicitly as a claim and justified immediately.

Phase 3. Derivation target.
Try to derive \( E2 \) inside the arbitrary magma satisfying \( E1 \).
If the consequences force the magma to be one-element, say so explicitly and conclude that \( E2 \) holds automatically.
If the consequences force the operation to be constant, substitute that into \( E2 \) explicitly and evaluate both sides.
Otherwise continue until the two sides of \( E2 \) are shown equal.

Phase 4. Counterexample search only if Phase 3 fails.
To refute implication, you must produce a magma \( M \) such that
\[
M \models E1 \quad\text{and}\quad M \not\models E2.
\]

For any candidate finite magma, the burden of proof is:
- first verify \( E1 \) globally in that magma;
- then give one explicit assignment witnessing failure of \( E2 \).

A single assignment may disprove \( M \models E2 \), but it never proves \( M \models E1 \).
